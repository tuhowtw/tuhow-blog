TU HOW(TU HAO) 2023/05/11

Being thick-skinned without bothering others could be called courageous. I want to become such a person who can speak up and take action when necessary.

![Sunset view from Building 3 of Waseda University!](/images/圖片.png)

Yesterday in the discussion class of Comparative Politics of East Asia, I had a heated argument with a classmate who believed that gender discrimination does not exist. He said, "Society was built by men, and it's natural for men to be in charge of working outside and women to take care of housde works”. He then asked the professor to explain whether gender discrimination really exists from a female perspective without any reason and context. Fortunately, the professor controlled the situation, and most of the classmates pointed out the weakness of his argument and how his rude language did not reflect an educated way of questioning. After all, the most important thing in a discussion class is expressing oneself and having a conversation based on context, not just expressing personal feelings.

I also participated in this debate and said, "Are you aware that many men also have a problem of talking too much?" I listened to him cursing and then wondered with other classmates about why he was mansplaining to us. This classmate was obviously thick-skinned and managed to steer the discussion in a poor way to express why he thought gender discrimination was a false issue. At the same time, he could be annoying because he didn't take into account other people's feelings.

Being thick-skined seems to be explainable as "daring to ask," but to what extent will demands make people feel disgusted? Where is the boundary between being able to demand and simply being rude? Perhaps it depends on whether the motive behind the demand is legitimate, and whether the way of demanding considers others. Being able to act for a legitimate purpose while respecting others' feelings is probably what bravery means.

Here are some counterexamples: It is admirable to be brave enough to start a conversation or initiate a topic with others, but if the purpose of starting a conversation is just to show superiority, then this person is probably just shameless and self-centered. For example, asking about someone's school and then saying, "If I were there, you would have to worry about not getting selected," or "I heard your school is very ordinary," if there are people who need to belittle others to build their confidence, then I can only feel sorry for them and hope that courage and confidence can be used for more meaningful purposes.

After yesterday's discussion, I found myself trembling, and it took a long time for me to calm down from my anger. I want to keep my courage/ energy for those who need it and to present arguments in a civilized way with others. That's why I like debating because discussing in a structured and rational language is much more interesting than emotional quarrel. I want to be someone that is gentle yet firm, and takes action for what matters.
